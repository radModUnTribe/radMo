## 1. The Core Thesis

Existing social media platforms didn't set out to radicalize anyone. They built systems that optimized for engagement, and engagement metrics happened to reward outrage, tribalism, and emotional manipulation. The algorithm didn't have values — it had a loss function. Polarization was a side effect.

RadMo proposes a different loss function.

The central insight is that **incentives, not moral appeals, drive behavior**. Platforms fail when they ask users to be better people. RadMo succeeds by making the incentive-aligned behavior the path of least resistance — by making intellectual honesty feel like status, cross-viewpoint engagement feel like rebellion, and epistemic quality feel like something worth competing for.

This is not a civic tech project. It is not a lecture. It is a platform designed to be more compelling than the alternatives because it routes existing human tendencies — status-seeking, tribal identity, dopamine loops — toward better collective epistemic outcomes.

**Crucially:** RadMo's incentives do not fight against human tendencies. They utilize them differently, in conjunction with what Tim Urban calls "higher mind" tendencies — the scientist in us that observes, theorizes, and tests rather than the lawyer who starts with a conclusion and works backward.

## 2. The Problem We're Solving

**The current information environment:**

Most Americans' relationship with news and political information follows this pattern:
1. Observe an event (through a tribal lens)
2. Estimate what their in-group thinks about it
3. Moderate their stated view in the direction of the group

This is the preference falsification dynamic — the Silence Tax. People publicly express views that diverge from what they privately believe because social pressure (real or perceived) makes honesty costly. The result: a public discourse that is more polarized than the actual distribution of private beliefs.

**The specific breakdown:**

- Roughly half of Americans will observe the same event and arrive at fundamentally different accounts of what happened — shaped by the lens of their political affiliation
- "Is" claims (what happened) and "ought" claims (what should happen) are routinely conflated, making factual disagreement indistinguishable from values disagreement
- The majority of political conflict is downstream of contested facts, not contested values — but our information environment makes it feel like all conflict is values-based
- Most media (mainstream and social) is optimized for engagement, which means optimized for emotional activation, which means optimized for the content that makes people feel their in-group is right and the out-group is threatening

**What "better" looks like:**

A better information environment is one where:
- What "is" — basic facts of what happened — is less contested
- Is/ought claims are clearly separated so people can agree on facts before disagreeing on values
- Cross-viewpoint exposure happens under conditions that reduce rather than increase polarization
- The quiet majority — people who privately hold more moderate views than they publicly express — have a platform and identity
- Less affective polarization (negative emotions toward outgroups) is the measurable outcome

## 3. Target Audience

### Primary: The Quiet Majority

More in Common's "hidden tribes" research estimates that approximately 67% of Americans are "exhausted majority" — people who find political debate exhausting, believe in compromise, and feel that extreme voices dominate the conversation. They hold more moderate views than they publicly express.

RadMo is built for them.

**Profile:**
- Frustrated with both mainstream media and social media's polarizing dynamics
- Privately moderate but publicly performing tribal loyalty out of social pressure
- Exhausted by political conversations at work, at family dinners, in social media feeds
- Intellectually curious, open to complexity, suspicious of hot takes
- Not apolitical — engaged, but not tribal

**Psychographic:** People who read something and think "this is more complicated than they're making it sound" — but don't have a platform where that instinct is rewarded.

### Secondary: The Reform-Minded News Consumer

People who already use Ground News, AllSides, or similar tools — who have demonstrated that they care about media literacy and are willing to pay for better information. They're already primed for RadMo's core value proposition; they just haven't had a social platform built for them.

### Generational tailwind

Younger voters (under 35) are showing pragmatic, issue-based behavior over tribal loyalty. In 2024 there were significant swings based on specific issue positions rather than party affiliation. This cohort is both the most targeted by algorithmic manipulation and the most skeptical of it — making them natural early adopters of a platform that treats them as capable of handling nuance.

### Who RadMo is NOT for (at launch)

- Hard partisans who derive identity from tribal conflict
- People who want a news tool without social features
- Users looking for validation of existing views

The platform will still be *usable* by these users, but the incentive structure won't reward their existing behavior — which means they're unlikely to stay.

## 4. Positioning

### The name

**Radical Moderate** — the oxymoron is the point.

- *Radical* = rebellious, edgy, committed, passionate
- *Moderate* = thoughtful, nuanced, reasonable
- Combined: "I'm passionately committed to thinking clearly"

It makes moderation feel like activism. Not fence-sitting. Not wishy-washy. Not weak. A choice. A bold one.

Cultural precedents that work the same way: "radical acceptance" (therapy), "radical honesty" (self-help). The word "radical" takes something soft and makes it hardcore.

### The identity

"Radically Moderate" gives the quiet majority a tribe and a posture. This matters strategically — people are tribal by nature. RadMo doesn't fight tribalism; it redirects it. The tribe is now "people who think clearly" vs. "institutions selling you information for profit rather than truth."

The enemy is not the left or the right. The enemy is the incentive structure that profits from keeping you outraged.

### Tagline options

- "Say what you actually think."
- "Join the radically moderate."
- "The hottest radically moderate take."
- "Where radical moderates think out loud."

"Say what you actually think." is currently the strongest — it directly addresses the Silence Tax and is an invitation, not a lecture.

## 5. Messaging Framework

### The narrative arc

**Step 1: Validation (not accusation)**

Don't tell users they were manipulated — that implies they were suckers. Instead, validate the instinct they already had:

> "Ever notice how reading the news makes you feel worse, not better informed? You're not imagining it. Here's why."

> "That weird feeling you get reading the news? Turns out, you were right."

> "Most media makes money by keeping you hooked, not informed. Outrage is profitable. You already suspected something was off."

**Step 2: Reframe the conflict**

Not left vs. right. Not red vs. blue.

> "You vs. companies selling you information for profit rather than truth."

This reframe is strategically important because it:
- Doesn't alienate anyone along political lines
- Gives RadMo a clear "villain" that doesn't require the user to be the bad guy
- Positions RadMo as the user's ally against a shared adversary

**Step 3: The invitation**

> "RadMo is where the people who are tired of performing for their side come to say what they actually think."

> "The quiet majority has been quiet long enough."

### What to avoid

- **Elitism:** Never imply that RadMo users are smarter or more enlightened. They're just tired.
- **Preachiness:** Never tell users what to think. Give them better tools and let them decide.
- **"Filter bubble" language:** Sounds condescending and implies users were passive victims.
- **"Both sides" framing:** Implies RadMo is politically neutral in a tepid centrist way. RadMo isn't neutral — it has a strong position on epistemic quality and is "radical" about it.

### Tone model: Tim Urban

Tim Urban (Wait But Why) is the tone reference. His framework for types of thinkers is directly relevant:

- **Scientists** — form views based on evidence, update when wrong
- **Lawyers** — start with a conclusion and build the case
- **Sports fans** — loyal to a team regardless of merit
- **Zealots** — identity is the ideology

RadMo is built for scientists. It's designed to make scientific thinking feel like the status play. But it communicates in a way accessible to everyone — no jargon, no condescension, humor as a vehicle for insight.

## 6. Competitive Landscape

### Direct competitors

**Ground News**
- Founded by Harleen Kaur (former NASA engineer) and Sukh Singh
- ~18 employees, estimated $5.7M ARR on ~$1M raised — exceptional capital efficiency
- News aggregation + left/center/right bias ratings
- Strong iOS app, growing subscriber base
- **Limitation:** News literacy tool only. No social layer. No community. Binary bias ratings that are themselves contested. No gamification.

**AllSides**
- Similar to Ground News but less polished
- Human-panel bias ratings, text-heavy, older audience
- **Limitation:** Same as Ground News — tool, not platform.

### Adjacent competitors

**X (formerly Twitter)**
- The dominant real-time public discourse platform
- Community Notes is their bridging feature — but the overall platform incentivizes outrage
- **RadMo insight:** Community Notes proves the bridging mechanic works at scale. X just can't deploy it as a platform principle because it would reduce engagement.

**Substack**
- Long-form writing platform with subscription model
- Growing political and intellectual commentary ecosystem
- No cross-viewpoint mechanics, no social graph around viewpoint diversity

**Reddit**
- Subreddit model creates ideological sorting
- Upvote/downvote system rewards in-group consensus
- No cross-viewpoint design

### Nonprofit / civic tech space

**Braver Angels, Living Room Conversations, Cortico, More in Common**
- All working on the same problem in various ways
- None have a consumer product at scale
- More in Common's "hidden tribes" research is the best academic grounding for RadMo's target audience
- These organizations are potential research partners, not competitors

### The key competitive gap

Every major platform (Meta, TikTok, YouTube) has now copied Community Notes as a **feature**. None has built a platform around the bridging **principle**. The feature is being commoditized. The platform layer is open.

Ground News and AllSides own the "media literacy tool" category. RadMo occupies the adjacent and currently empty category: **the social platform built around epistemic quality**.

## 7. Differentiation

### What makes RadMo genuinely different

**1. The social layer on top of the problem**
RadMo is not a tool you consult. It's a platform you live in. The social mechanics — posting, following, the credibility score, the popup feature — make epistemic quality a social activity, not a solo act of discipline.

**2. Credibility as status**
Replacing likes and follower counts with a credibility score that reflects epistemic quality is a genuine mechanic shift. Status-seeking is one of the highest-dopamine social activities. If credibility becomes the status metric, users will optimize for it.

**3. Multi-dimensional bridging**
Community Notes' binary left/right model is a known limitation. RadMo's cross-cultural, cross-geographic, cross-generational model is a genuine evolution — not just a political spectrum but a rich multi-dimensional model of perspective diversity.

**4. Is/ought separation as a product feature**
No existing platform makes the factual/normative distinction visible. RadMo's is/ought scoring is a genuinely novel feature with no direct competitor.

**5. The cooling period**
Deliberately less addictive than alternatives. This is both a product differentiator and a values signal — the platform is willing to sacrifice engagement for epistemic quality.

**6. Transparent business model**
Subscription over advertising is increasingly a differentiator as users become more aware of engagement-driven advertising models. "We make money when you find this valuable, not when you're outraged" is a trust signal that no ad-supported platform can match.

### What RadMo is NOT trying to do

- Replace Twitter/X for real-time breaking news
- Replace Reddit for community discussion
- Replace Ground News for media literacy tools
- Be a fact-checking service

RadMo is a social platform where the incentive structure rewards intellectual honesty and cross-viewpoint engagement. Everything else is in service of that.

## 8. Go-To-Market Thinking

*Early stage — directional only, not finalized*

### Phase 1: Seed the credibility culture

The biggest cold-start problem: credibility scores and cross-viewpoint validation only work if there are users with different perspectives actively rating each other. A platform with only one type of user produces no signal.

**Early approaches:**
- Invite users explicitly from across the political/cultural spectrum from day one
- Consider seeding with "founding members" who represent the Quiet Majority — people already in public life who are known for nuanced thinking (journalists, academics, independent writers)
- Partner with More in Common, Braver Angels, or similar organizations to access their existing communities of bridge-builders

### Phase 2: Build the identity

"Radically Moderate" needs to feel like a real thing people want to be — not a product category. Community-building before scale:
- The "Radically Moderate Takes" thread format as a shareable content unit
- Leaderboards of high-credibility contributors (public, opt-in)
- A clear in-group identity that people can express externally ("I post on RadMo")

### Phase 3: Drive network effects through friction

Counterintuitively, the cooling period and credibility gating may drive word-of-mouth better than frictionless onboarding. People recommend things that made them think differently. RadMo's slower, more considered experience is the product.

### Target channels

- **Word of mouth from the frustrated moderate:** Primary. The "I finally found somewhere I can say what I actually think" moment is highly shareable.
- **Newsletter / Substack ecosystem:** High overlap with RadMo's target audience
- **Workplace frustration angle:** "Tired of tiptoeing around politics at work?" — a specific, resonant pain point
- **Podcast ecosystem:** The Tim Urban / Sam Harris / Freakonomics listener is a natural RadMo early adopter

## 9. Long-Term Vision

The long-term ambition is not just a platform — it is cultural change.

**Near-term (product):** A platform where the Quiet Majority has a home, intellectual honesty earns status, and the is/ought distinction is part of everyday information consumption.

**Medium-term (cultural):** The "Radically Moderate" identity becomes a cultural marker — a position people identify with publicly, the way "I'm an independent" became a political identity in the 20th century.

**Long-term (political):** Influence governmental policy through cultural change. If RadMo can demonstrably shift the information environment toward less affective polarization and better epistemic quality, it becomes an argument for platform regulation — and a proof of concept that better incentive structures produce better outcomes.

The generational tailwind is real. Younger voters are showing issue-based pragmatism over tribal loyalty. The platform that captures that cohort and gives them a shared identity and vocabulary will have outsized cultural influence.

## 10. Open Strategic Questions

- [ ] **Defining success metrics:** What does "better information environment" look like as a measurable outcome? Affective polarization scores? Cross-viewpoint engagement rates? Is/ought ratio improvements over time? Need concrete KPIs before scale.

- [ ] **Cold-start problem:** How do we ensure the first users represent sufficient viewpoint diversity for the credibility and bridging mechanics to produce signal? What's the minimum viable diversity threshold?

- [ ] **Monetization timing:** When does subscription pricing begin? What's the freemium threshold? Free with limited credibility features vs. full access?

- [ ] **The "More in Common" partnership angle:** Their hidden tribes research directly validates RadMo's thesis. Is there a formal research partnership or data-sharing opportunity?

- [ ] **International expansion:** The binary left/right limitation of Community Notes is most acute in non-American contexts where political spectrums are more complex. RadMo's multi-dimensional model is inherently more globally applicable. When and how does international enter the roadmap?

- [ ] **The moderation problem:** A platform that rewards intellectual honesty and cross-viewpoint engagement will still attract bad-faith actors who game the system. What's the moderation philosophy and what can be automated vs. human-reviewed?

- [ ] **Competitive response:** If RadMo gains traction, Ground News could add social features, or X could deepen Community Notes into a platform. How does RadMo build defensible moats?
