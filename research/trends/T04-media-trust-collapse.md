# T04: Trust in News Is Declining; News Avoidance Is Rising

## Summary
Global trust in news has been declining for years and is now at historically low levels. Simultaneously, a growing share of people are actively avoiding news — not from apathy but from exhaustion, anxiety, and a sense that news is weaponized.

## Strategic Relevance
This is the market opportunity. Declining trust in legacy media creates demand for a new kind of information platform with transparent incentives. Radical Moderate's subscription model and explicit anti-outrage design are direct responses to *why* people are tuning out. The framing of "you vs. companies selling you information for profit" maps directly onto these findings.

## Key Findings
- Trust in news globally sits at ~40% (Reuters DNR 2024), down from 44% at the height of COVID in 2020 and 10+ points below 2017 levels
- News avoidance has risen from 29% in 2017 to 39% in 2024 (sometimes or often avoiding news), a 10-point increase over seven years
- In the UK, interest in news has nearly halved since 2015 (70% → 38%); news avoidance nearly doubled (24% → 46%)
- Top reasons for news avoidance: content feels repetitive, anxiety-inducing, and makes people feel powerless — not lack of time
- The 2025 Reuters DNR (n=~100,000, 48 countries) notes younger audiences say news feels "confusing, overwhelming, and far removed from their lives"
- Trust has declined more in media environments where TV news use has declined and social media news use has grown — suggesting the problem is structural, not just cyclical
- People think social media *divides* them (-13 net), while messaging apps *connect* them (+19 net) — perception of platform intent matters
