# T06: Outrage Amplification Is a Feature, Not a Bug, of Ad-Based Platforms

## Summary
Engagement-based ranking algorithms — the standard model for ad-supported social platforms — systematically amplify content that provokes strong emotional reactions, including outrage and partisan animosity. This is not a side effect but a direct result of optimizing for engagement as a proxy for value.

## Strategic Relevance
This is the core of the "you vs. companies selling you information for profit" reframe. The business model IS the product; ad-based platforms have a structural incentive to polarize. This makes Radical Moderate's subscription model a values statement, not just a revenue choice.

## Key Findings
- Engagement-based ranking algorithms (Twitter/X, TikTok, Facebook) predict engagement metrics — retweets, replies, watch time — and rank content that maximizes these; this systematically surfaces emotionally charged, partisan content (PNAS Nexus, 2025)
- Engagement-based timelines on Twitter showed significantly more partisan content than stated-preference (chronological) timelines; the amplification primarily came from out-group political content — meaning the algorithm surfaces your political opponents' most inflammatory content (PNAS Nexus, 2025)
- Politicians and content creators report feeling "trapped" by these incentives; those who tried to tone down inflammatory content saw metrics collapse (Senator Ben Sasse, cited in Knight First Amendment Institute review)
- The Piccardi et al. (2025) LLM-based classifier for "anti-democratic attitudes and partisan animosity" (AAPA) provides a working technical definition of what platforms could choose to downrank but don't — because it would reduce engagement
- Overperception of moral outrage online: people consistently overestimate how much outrage exists in networks, which inflates beliefs about intergroup hostility (Brady et al., 2023, *Nature Human Behaviour*)
