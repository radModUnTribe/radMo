# Trend 01: Affective Polarization Is Rising

## Strategic Relevance
RadMo's core value proposition is reducing affective polarization — not ideological polarization. This distinction matters: people's *policy positions* haven't diverged nearly as much as their *feelings* about the other side. The problem is social and emotional, not purely intellectual. This validates a platform that targets the emotional incentive structure rather than trying to change minds through argument.

## Core Finding
Affective polarization — the degree to which partisans dislike and distrust the opposing party — has intensified significantly in the United States over the past few decades. Unlike ideological polarization (actual policy divergence), affective polarization has spread even among citizens whose policy views remain moderate. It spills into non-political domains: hiring decisions, dating behavior, and social relationships.

## Key Nuance
Increasing affective polarization is **primarily an American story**. Cross-national research finds that while some countries show similar trends, the US shows the steepest and most consistent rise. This suggests something specific about the American media and political context — not just a universal social media effect.

## Caveats
- Most research is US-focused; generalizability to other countries is limited
- Causality between social media use and affective polarization is hard to establish cleanly
- Some studies find affective polarization increases civic participation, which complicates the "it's purely bad" framing

## RadMo Implications
- The platform should target the *emotional* experience of political engagement, not just information exposure
- Framing should address the gap between how hostile people *perceive* the other side to be vs. how hostile they *actually* are — this misperception gap is a lever
- The US context is the right starting market given the severity of the problem here
