# T02: Cross-Viewpoint Exposure Alone Often Backfires

## Summary
Simply showing people opposing political views — without the right conditions — often increases polarization rather than reducing it. This is one of the most counterintuitive and strategically important findings for Radical Moderate's product design.

## Strategic Relevance
This directly constrains product design. A platform that just forces left–right contact without quality conditions will make things worse. The *how* of exposure matters more than the *fact* of it. This justifies the emphasis on gamification, contact quality conditions, and the "someone like you / unlike you" popup framing.

## Key Findings
- In a large Twitter field experiment, exposing users to an opposing-party bot feed did not reduce polarization; Republicans showed increased polarization (Bail et al., 2018, *PNAS*)
- Exposure to cross-cutting political information on social media can trigger "defensive mechanisms" that solidify ideological rigidity, especially when algorithms amplify emotional reactions to opposing content (Asian Journal of Public Opinion Research, 2024)
- The echo chamber hypothesis may be partially wrong: it is not isolation from opposing views but cross-space interaction via digital media that drives partisan sorting, because it erases local heterogeneity and aligns conflicts along national partisan lines (Törnberg, 2022, *PNAS*)
- Under specific conditions — less hostile contexts, active seeking of diverse viewpoints, deliberate engagement — exposure to opposing perspectives *can* reduce polarization (Fishkin et al., 2021; Hartman et al., 2022; cited in AJPOR 2024)
- News coverage *about* polarization increases perceived polarization more strongly than exposure to partisan media itself (Tsfati et al., 2024, *Political Communication*)

## Confidence
**High.** Bail et al. (2018) is one of the most cited social media / polarization studies in existence. The nuance — that quality conditions matter — is also well-supported. Design implications are clear.
