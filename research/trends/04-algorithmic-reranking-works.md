# Trend 04: Downranking Outrage Produces Measurable Attitude Change

## Strategic Relevance
This is the direct proof of concept for RadMo's core algorithmic approach. If the problem is that algorithms amplify outrage, and if reducing that amplification demonstrably shifts attitudes, then a platform built around a different ranking philosophy is not just philosophically appealing — it's empirically validated. This is the key "it works" evidence.

## Core Finding
A preregistered field experiment (N=1,256) during the 2024 US presidential campaign used a browser extension to rerank participants' X/Twitter feeds in real time. Reducing exposure to antidemocratic attitudes and partisan animosity (AAPA) shifted out-party animosity by more than **2 points on a 100-point feeling thermometer** — in both directions depending on whether exposure was increased or decreased. Effects were consistent across party lines. This provides the first platform-independent causal evidence that feed ranking directly alters affective polarization.

## Why This Matters
Previous research on algorithmic effects (e.g., the Facebook News Feed experiments) either required platform cooperation or relied on correlational data. This study was conducted without Twitter/X cooperation, establishing an independent methodology — and importantly, a methodology that doesn't require Big Tech to change its behavior for the effect to be studied.

## Caveats
- The 2-point shift on a 100-point scale is statistically significant but modest — this is a short-term effect over 10 days, not long-term attitude change
- The experiment modified existing feeds rather than testing a new platform from scratch; ecological validity to RadMo's context is indirect
- "Attitude change" (feeling thermometer scores) is not the same as behavioral change (less hostile behavior, more cross-partisan cooperation)

## RadMo Implications
- This is the strongest single scientific citation for RadMo's pitch: "we know algorithmic intervention works"
- The effect was symmetric across party lines — no evidence one side is more responsive, which is important for non-partisan positioning
- The 10-day timeframe suggests effects accumulate with consistent use — a subscription platform with daily engagement has structural advantages
- This also validates the "cooling period" feature concept: reducing the immediate emotional charge of content has measurable downstream effects
