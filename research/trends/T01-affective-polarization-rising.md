# T01: US Affective Polarization Is Rising

## Summary
Americans increasingly dislike and distrust people from the opposing political party — not just disagree with their policies. This "affective" (emotional) polarization has grown steadily and is more pronounced in the US than in most other democracies.

## Strategic Relevance
This is the core problem Radical Moderate exists to address. Understanding its scale and trajectory is essential for sizing the market, crafting the mission narrative, and anticipating objections.

## Key Findings
- The US stands out globally for the pace of its long-term increase in affective polarization (Boxell, Gentzkow, Shapiro, 2021 — analysis of 12 democracies from the late 1970s)
- Affective polarization among the full electorate (not just partisans) is increasing most notably in the US and Germany; most other democracies are stable or declining (Reiljan & Ryan, 2023, *Public Opinion Quarterly*)
- Internet access has contributed causally to rising affective polarization; the mechanism is increased news consumption and partisan identity salience, not just echo chambers (Lelkes et al., 2017)
- Partisan animosity has intensified and become more personal: Pew found majorities in both parties view the opposing party as a threat to the nation's well-being (Pew Research, 2019)
- Affective polarization has real-world consequences: during COVID-19, partisan animosity shaped pandemic attitudes and behaviors independent of local outbreak severity (Druckman et al., 2020, *Nature Human Behaviour*)
