# T03: Algorithmic Downranking of Outrage Reduces Polarization

## Summary
Reducing users' exposure to posts expressing partisan animosity and anti-democratic attitudes measurably reduces affective polarization — equivalent to years of natural attitude drift. This is a direct causal intervention, not a correlation.

## Strategic Relevance
This is the scientific basis for the claim that the platform's reward/ranking algorithm is the product. It validates the core thesis that changing *incentive structures*, not just providing information, is the lever. It also suggests that what gets downranked matters — it's not just about showing diverse content but about reducing hostile content.

## Key Findings
- A Stanford-led field experiment (n=1,256, 10 days, during the 2024 election) downranked posts expressing anti-democratic attitudes and partisan animosity (AAPA) using an LLM classifier; exposed users showed measurably warmer attitudes toward the opposing party — a ~2-point shift on a 100-point feeling thermometer (Piccardi et al., 2025, *Science*)
- The effect was bipartisan: improvements held for both liberals and conservatives
- Downranking also reduced anger and sadness while scrolling; upranking increased them
- The 2-point shift is comparable to the estimated total change in attitudes across the US population over a decade
- Method was platform-independent (browser extension), demonstrating this can be done without platform cooperation
- No posts were removed — only reranked; this is important for free speech framing
- Contrasting finding: simply switching Facebook to a chronological feed did NOT reduce polarization, suggesting that crude algorithmic changes aren't sufficient — targeted content downranking is what matters (Guess et al., 2023, *Science*)

## Confidence
**Medium-High.** The Piccardi et al. (2025) study is rigorous and the first of its kind with platform-independent methodology. Effect size is modest but consistent. Replication needed; short-term effects (10 days) may not persist long-term.
